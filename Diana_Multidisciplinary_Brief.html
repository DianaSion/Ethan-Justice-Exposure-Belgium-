Annex A — Chemistry Foundations (for reviewers outside chemistry)

1) Lewis Structures, Formal Charge, Resonance

  • Lewis method: count valence e⁻ → connect skeleton → satisfy octets (H = duet) → add multiple bonds/charges as needed.
  • Formal charge: FC = (valence e⁻) − (nonbonding e⁻ + ½·bonding e⁻). Prefer lower FCs placed on appropriate atoms.
  • Resonance: contributors are bookkeeping; real structure is a delocalized electron density. Explains equalized bonds (e.g., benzene, carboxylate).
  • Hypervalency via 3-center–4-electron (3c–4e) bonds: rationalizes “expanded octet” species (I3⁻, XeF2) without invoking literal d-orbital valence.

3) Hybridization (qualitative)

  • sp (2 domains, linear); sp² (3, trigonal planar); sp³ (4, tetrahedral).
  • σ bonds = end-on overlap (stronger); π bonds = side-on p-overlap (planarity constraints, conjugation).
  • Use 3c–4e model for many “sp³d/sp³d²” hypervalent cases.

4) Molecular Orbital (MO) Theory

  • LCAO: atomic orbitals combine → bonding (lower energy) and antibonding (higher, *) MOs.
  • Bond order = (e⁻ in bonding − e⁻ in antibonding)/2.
  • Explains O₂ paramagnetism (two unpaired e⁻ in π*), color/conductivity trends, and heteronuclear polarity.

5) From Molecules to Solids (Band Theory)

  • As atoms N→∞, closely spaced MOs form bands.
  • Insulators (diamond): full valence band, large gap; semiconductors (Si): small gap; metals (Fe, Cu alloys): partially filled band → conductivity.

6) Biological Links (where bonding becomes life)

  • Peptide bond resonance → planarity & secondary structure (α/β).
  • DNA/RNA: H-bond pairing + π–π stacking; phosphate backbone involves hypervalent P descriptions.
  • Water networks: H-bond lattice → anomalous properties essential for biology (heat capacity, density max at 4 °C).
  • Chromophores: conjugation length controls absorption (retinal, flavins, chlorophyll).
